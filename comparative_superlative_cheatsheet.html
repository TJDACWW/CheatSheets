📚 Comparative & Superlative Forms

BBC Learning English - Grammar Reference Guide

🎯 Meaning and Use
Comparatives: Compare Two Things
Use comparative adjectives and adverbs to compare one person or thing with another.
"Today is hotter than yesterday."
"I think documentaries are more interesting than the news."
"Can you drive faster? – I'm late."
"My brother speaks more fluently than me."
Superlatives: Compare with Several Things
Use superlative adjectives and adverbs to compare one person or thing with several others of the same kind.
"September is the busiest month for our business."
"Angelina Jolie was the highest paid actress last year."
"Which student has worked the hardest this year?"
🔧 Formation Rules
One Syllable: Add -er / -est
For adjectives and adverbs with one syllable, add -er (comparative) and -est (superlative).

Comparative
Superlative

📝 Spelling Rule:
If the adjective already ends in -e, just add -r (comparative) or -st (superlative)
Vowel + Consonant: Double the Last Letter
If an adjective ends in vowel + consonant, double the last letter before adding -er or -est (unless it ends in -w).

Three+ Syllables: Use more / most
If an adjective or adverb has three or more syllables, use more (comparative) and most (superlative).

Two Syllables: Variable Rules
Two-syllable adjectives can use either method for some words:

Two Syllables Ending in -y: Change y to i
For two-syllable adjectives or adverbs ending in -y, change the 'y' to 'i' before adding -er/-est.

🌟 Special Patterns & Usage

Repeated Comparatives

Use comparative + and + comparative to show how something changes:

"Train journeys in the UK are becoming more and more expensive."
"The sky grew darker and darker and we knew the storm would break soon."

The...the... Pattern

Use the + comparative, the + comparative to show how one thing changes as another changes:

"The faster they went, the louder they screamed."
"The taller she grew, the thinner she became."

Superlatives + Present Perfect

Often use superlatives with present perfect to talk about life experiences:

"That was the funniest film I've seen this year."
"He swam the fastest he has ever swum to win gold."

Emphasis with much/a lot

Use much or a lot before comparatives for emphasis:

"Shirley is much more popular than Janet."
"This winter is a lot worse than last year."
"I work much more effectively when I have a good night's sleep."
🔄 As...As Constructions
Equal Comparison: as + adjective/adverb + as
To say that two things or people are similar in some way:
"Mario is as clever as his brother."
"Juliana shouted as loudly as the others but the teacher didn't choose her."
Unequal Comparison: not as/so + adjective + as
To say that two things or people are not equal in some way:
"Ishbel is not as talented as her sister."
"The final wasn't so exciting as the semi-final."
Common Idioms with as...as
Set expressions often used in narrative texts:
"He was as quiet as a mouse."
"The material was as thin as ice."
"Her hands were as warm as toast."
"He ran as fast as the wind to escape."
"When Anna heard the news, she turned as white as a sheet."
"He picked her up and she was as light as a feather."
⚡ Quick Reference
Key Points to Remember
With Comparatives:
• Usually use "than"
• Sometimes no "than" needed
• Can use "much/a lot" for emphasis
With Superlatives:
• Usually use "the"
• Sometimes use possessive adjectives
• Great with present perfect tense
Special Forms:
• Irregular forms must be memorized
• Two-syllable rules vary
• Watch spelling changes
